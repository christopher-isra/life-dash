Setting The Scene
The core insight behind all of this is simple. People with ongoing health conditions generate data their whole lives — prescriptions, blood tests, annual checkups — but they have no way to see it as a story. Every result arrives as a single number, in a different portal, with no connection to anything else.

Life Dash solves that. Free, private, no doctor required. You log your meds, upload your results, and for the first time you can see whether anything is actually changing.

What we're trying to do with these ads is make that problem feel real before we offer the solution.
Talking Points
  • We're not selling a diagnosis — we're selling visibility
  • The audience already has the data, they just can't use it
  • Free removes the biggest objection before they even have it
Why Now
The closest competitor in this space — Heads Up Health — has quietly exited the consumer market and gone B2B. That leaves a significant gap. There is no well-known, well-marketed consumer product that does what Life Dash does.

On top of that, we have a specific wave to ride: the GLP-1 generation. Millions of people are now a year or more into metabolic medication and have never once seen their bloodwork trends alongside their dosage. They're the perfect early adopter — highly motivated, medically engaged, and underserved by existing tools.

These three campaign ideas are built around the three clearest entry points into that audience.
Talking Points
  • Heads Up Health pivot = first-mover advantage for consumer positioning
  • GLP-1 users are a natural beachhead — high intent, trackable cohort
  • Cholesterol and general med tracking follow the same emotional arc
Idea 01

Medication & Lab Results

The broadest idea. Catches anyone managing ongoing prescriptions or bloodwork. No condition named — just the universal frustration of scattered health data.

01
Idea 01 · Three Paths
This idea has three executions, and they escalate in boldness.

Path A is the quietest. Just a split-frame — paper on one side, a clean graph on the other. The headline does the work: "See If It's Working." Four words that ask the question millions of people have never been able to answer. We're betting that question alone stops the scroll.

Path B is warmer. We're in the kitchen — morning pills, coffee, phone on the counter. It's about agency: you already have the routine, Life Dash adds the picture. Headline: "Your Medication. Your Results. Connected." It reassures rather than provokes.

Path C is the most confrontational. The before/after diptych — paper chaos on the left, clean dashboard on the right. Same hands in both frames. The headline earns its specificity: "Six Portals. One Dashboard. Free." That number six is real. Anyone who's tried to manage their health across NHS, private, and pharmacy portals will feel it immediately.
Which to Lead With
  • Path A for cold audiences — lowest barrier, highest emotional hook
  • Path C for retargeting — they already know the pain, now name it precisely
  • Path B for brand warmth and Instagram Stories format
Idea 02

The GLP-1 Generation

Medication as the hero. No condition naming — Meta policy compliance built in. The story is metabolic change over six months, not weight loss.

02
Idea 02 · Three Paths
This is our highest-intent audience and potentially our most shareable creative.

Path A is a data story. Two dashboard screenshots — Week 1 versus Week 24. Sparse versus rich. The headline is "Beyond The Scale" — which is both the creative concept and the insight. Everyone on GLP-1 is obsessing over the scale. We're the only product asking what's happening everywhere else.

Path B shifts to empowerment. We put the patient in front of the doctor — but the patient is the one with the data. Shot from behind their shoulder. Headline: "Walk In Already Knowing." That's a fantasy every person with a chronic condition has had. We're making it real.

Path C is pure product. A multi-line graph — cholesterol, blood sugar, liver markers, weight — all tracking together. The question in the headline, "What Is GLP-1 Doing?", is the thing people are Googling at 11pm. We answer it.
Compliance Note
  • We do not name any condition in any GLP-1 execution
  • No before/after body imagery — all data and people, no physique comparison
  • Meta health advertising policy reviewed — all three paths are clean
Idea 03

Cholesterol Treadmill

For the statin taker who's been compliant for years and has no idea if it's working. The emotional hook is proof of effort, not fear of disease.

03
Idea 03 · Three Paths
This idea is built on a quiet frustration that almost nobody is speaking to.

Path A is the most human. A man in his late 50s at a kitchen table, holding his phone. Behind him — slightly out of focus — a family photo. The graph on his screen shows three years of cholesterol readings. He's been doing the right thing. He just never knew it was working. Headline: "Three Years. One Graph."

Path B is high-contrast and bold. A printed lab result with LDL 187 circled. Overlaid: the same number at the start of an 18-month graph that trends down to 114. The headline: "18 Months Of Truth." It reframes the printout — not as a verdict, but as a starting point.

Path C flips the doctor's waiting room. Usually that moment is anxious — you're waiting to be told something. Here, the patient is already comparing her new results to her Life Dash trend. She already knows. The headline is a play on words that earns its place: "No Waiting To Know."
Casting Note
  • All three paths need ages 45–65, diverse, real — not aspirational
  • Path A: the quiet confidence of someone who did the unglamorous work
  • Path C: a woman leading — she owns the data, the doctor confirms it
Second Creative Direction
Alongside the photo-based work, we've developed a parallel set of text-and-graphic executions — no photography at all. Just the headline, the brand, and a data visual.

These are faster to produce, easier to iterate, and in many cases will outperform the photo versions on cold traffic because there's no human to misread or scroll past. The message lands in under a second.

Each headline was put through an Ogilvy lens first — brand name in the headline, specific promise, named audience — then compressed using the Seven Sweeps framework to strip anything that wasn't load-bearing. What's left is as short as it can be without losing meaning.
Why Test Both
  • Photo builds brand trust and warmth — better for mid-funnel
  • Text/graphic drives click intent — better for cold acquisition
  • Running both gives us signal on which emotion is doing the work
Text & Graphic · The Headlines
Nine headlines, each one built from a longer Ogilvy source line and then cut to the bone.

Idea 1: "See If It's Working" — the question. "Your Medication. Your Results. Connected." — the promise. "Six Portals. One Dashboard. Free." — the specific pain.

Idea 2: "Beyond The Scale" — the aspiration without clinical framing. "Walk In Already Knowing" — pure confidence. "What Is GLP-1 Doing?" — the question people are already asking.

Idea 3: "Three Years. One Graph." — proof of effort made visible. "18 Months Of Truth" — the trend they never got to see. "No Waiting To Know" — the pun that stops the read and earns its place.

Any one of these can run as a static card on Meta or a Google Responsive Search Ad headline tomorrow. No shoot required.
First Tests to Run
  • "See If It's Working" vs "Six Portals. One Dashboard. Free." — question vs specific pain
  • "Beyond The Scale" — test with GLP-1 interest targeting only
  • "No Waiting To Know" — doctor's waiting room context makes the pun land
