Life Dash

Photo-Based Campaign

What to say, idea by idea. The pitch, the logic, and the close.

April 2026
How to Open
"What we've built here is three separate entry points into the same audience — people who are managing their health long-term and have never once been able to see whether it's working. Every piece of creative we're going to look at is built around a moment. Not a product feature, not a benefit statement — a moment the person already knows. A kitchen table. A doctor's office. A piece of paper they've held too many times. Our job in this meeting is to decide which of those moments we back first."
Why Photo-Based
"Photo-based creative earns something text alone can't — it puts the product in someone's life before they've agreed to care about it. The person scrolling doesn't have to imagine anything. They see a kitchen table that looks like their kitchen table. They see a waiting room they've sat in. Recognition is faster than persuasion. That's the bet with this creative direction. We're not explaining what Life Dash does — we're showing someone who already has it."

If challenged: "The text-based work is a parallel track — we'll cover that separately. This direction is specifically about building brand familiarity with cold audiences at the top of funnel."

Idea 01

Medication & Lab Results

01
Idea 01 · Path A — What to Say
"This is the broadest idea and the simplest execution. A split-frame still — paper on one side, a graph on the other. No person in it. The headline is four words: 'See If It's Working.'

The reason I like leading with this is that it doesn't ask anything of the audience. It doesn't require them to identify with a condition, a demographic, a lifestyle. It just asks the question that anyone on any long-term medication has had in the back of their mind for years. If they stop on that question, we've done the hard part. The product answers it."

Objection — 'too vague': "Vague is intentional here. We're casting the widest net at the top of the funnel. The specificity comes in retargeting."

Idea 01 · Path B — What to Say
"Path B is warmer. We're in the kitchen — morning pills, coffee, phone on the counter. The shift here is from anxiety to agency. We're not telling someone they have a problem. We're showing someone who already has their life sorted and we're the piece that makes it complete.

This one is better suited to people who are already organised about their health — they take their pills, they see their GP, they just don't have visibility. The headline connects two things they already own: their medication and their results. The word 'connected' is the entire product promise."

Best placement: "Instagram Stories and Facebook Feed. The flat-lay format is native to those placements and it reads as content, not an ad."

Idea 01 · Path C — What to Say
"Path C is the most aggressive of the three and I think it's the strongest retargeting piece we have. Before-and-after diptych — genuine chaos on the left, clean dashboard on the right. The thing that makes it work is the specificity: six portals.

Anyone who's actually tried to manage their health across multiple providers — NHS, private, pharmacy — will count to six in their head before they finish reading that headline. That's the moment. Not the design, not the graph — that moment of recognition. We don't need to explain the product after that."

Production note to raise: "The video version — papers dissolving into a graph — is the priority execution here. If budget forces a choice, this is the one to animate first."

Idea 02

The GLP-1 Generation

02
Idea 02 · Path A — What to Say
"GLP-1 is the most defined audience we have — high intent, medically engaged, and almost completely underserved by existing tools. The whole market is obsessing over one number. We're the only product asking about everything else.

Path A shows two dashboard screenshots — week one versus week twenty-four. Sparse versus rich. The headline is two words: 'Beyond The Scale.' We're not positioning against weight loss. We're expanding what success looks like. That's a completely open space right now and it's ours if we move first."

Compliance note to pre-empt: "No condition is named anywhere in this execution. Meta health policy reviewed — it's clean."

Idea 02 · Path B — What to Say
"This is the most emotionally powerful creative in the entire set. A patient showing a doctor their own data. Shot from behind the patient — they're the one leading the conversation.

Every person with a long-term condition has sat in that room not knowing what they're about to be told. This ad shows them the other version of that moment — where they walk in already knowing. That fantasy is universally relatable. We're not selling a product feature, we're selling a feeling that most patients have never had. That's the pitch."

Casting direction to flag: "The patient must lead this scene. If the shot reads as a doctor explaining something to a patient, it kills the whole concept."

Idea 02 · Path C — What to Say
"Path C is the most product-forward of the GLP-1 set. A close-up of a multi-line graph — four markers tracked together. One dips, the next follows. The headline is the question people are already Googling: 'What Is GLP-1 Doing?'

We don't answer it in the ad. The graph implies the answer exists — but only inside the product. That gap is the click. This one also pairs well with Google search — run it alongside intent keywords and you're showing up for the exact question the person just typed."
Idea 03

Cholesterol Treadmill

03
Idea 03 · Path A — What to Say
"Idea three targets a frustration nobody is currently advertising to. Millions of people take a statin every morning and have no idea if it's doing anything. Not because they don't care — because nobody's ever shown them.

Path A is a man at his kitchen table. Phone showing a two-year graph. Family photo blurred in the background. The emotional payoff isn't fear — it's proof of effort. He did the unglamorous work. He took the pill every day. We're the first thing that's ever shown him it worked. That's a profound moment and we've made it quiet, which is exactly right."
Idea 03 · Path B — What to Say
"Path B is high contrast — a lab printout with a single number highlighted: 187. Overlaid with a Life Dash graph showing where that number went over 18 months.

The headline reframes the printout. '18 Months Of Truth' is saying: that piece of paper you held in the car park outside the GP's office was not the whole story. The whole story is what happened after — and for the first time, you can see it. The 18-month number is deliberately odd. It feels like a real person's experience, not a marketing round number."
Idea 03 · Path C — What to Say
"Path C flips the waiting room. The anxiety is gone. She's sitting there comparing her new lab results to a trend she's been watching for a year. The doctor walks in and she's already nodding.

The headline is a play on words — 'No Waiting To Know.' You read it two ways: no waiting, and no — waiting to know. That double-take is the scroll stop. It's the only wordplay in the entire set and it earns its place. The emotion is quiet confidence, not urgency. That's a tone most health advertising never reaches."
